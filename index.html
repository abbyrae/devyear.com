A COMMUNITY OF WEBMAKERS

When planning Dev.Year, the easy thing would have been to put together 9 months of classes, content, students and teachers, push the start button and watch it all happen. Instead, we want to invite you to join us in building something better: a community of people committed to learning and building the web together. We'll devote our time to giving you the resources you need to build your skills, connect you with mentors, and match you with projects to work on. Our goal is for Dev.Year to be the beginning of your life's work, not just some career training.


You'll start learning git, Ruby, JS, etc. as you need them but, more importantly, we want you to be equipped to learn what doesn't exist yet. By the end of the year, we want you to become a bona fide member of an open-source team. Most importantly, we hope that you'll fall in love with a problem or a project that will keep you busy after our year is over. During the year, our mentors will be available to advise the teams you form. Instead of filling your time with classes and homework, you'll be busy discussing FOSS over dinner, running hack-a-thons, going to workshops, user groups and conferences, coding with your mentor, and building projects with a team.
When Dev.Year ends, we hope the transition is unnoticeable. Instead of going off to the "real world" you will have already been in it, living and working alongside other developers. Our focus is not just on career preparation, but to set you free to build the open web or join a company that is solving a problem you care about. Oh, and the year costs us (and you) just $4995 per student.

As Thoreau says in Walden, "Let's not play life, or study it merely, while the community supports [us] at this expensive game, but earnestly live it from beginning to end."

THE PROGRAM & COSTS

Instead of a schedule of classes, we'll put you into a community of developers, give you a fieldbook, and help you build projects. Your tuition money won't go to pay for "teachers" in the traditional sense, instead you will support the community of experienced developers that have committed to working with you all year. Rather than dictating the schedule for you each week, you will have a lot of say in what you learn next and what projects take priority. The core of the schedule revolves around a commitment by you, our staff, and the developer community to work together every day for a year. Members of Dev.Year will commit to keeping Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays from 8:00 am - 1:00 pm, and Tuesdays and Thursdays from 9:00 am - 10:00 pm, open for programming. All new members of Dev.Year commit to contributing a minimum of $70/week (for 36 weeks) on Gittip to support mentors, guests, and workshop leaders that are part of the Dev.Year community. New members will also pay $2475 to cover hard costs like our main facilitator's salary, rent, and insurance. The total cost of Dev.Year is $4995. Additionally, group meals are an important part of our weekly schedule. To save money and help with planning, new members will pay for 3 weekly meals in advance ($27/week). If you run the totals, it averages out to about $660 per month with everything: food, hard costs, and Gittip support. We're really excited about using Gittip to run more of an Open School that invites in the entire web development community to work with us. Not only does this structure closely connect us with the work of great open source developers, it allows those developers greater freedom to invest in your education on their own terms. Because of this, you will get to work with a lot of amazing people who wouldn't normally sign up to teach in a classroom setting. Perhaps most interesting about this model is that it allows members to stay on after Dev.Year ends and transition into being a supported member of the community. Join our community of webmakers and begin your life's work.

THE SCHEDULE


COMMUNITY

You'll meet twice weekly to collaborate with your team. Join the broader community by getting active on GitHub, mailing lists, twitter, and IRC. Attend Meetups, user groups, and conferences with your team. We'll have developers joining us every week during our co-working sessions.

MENTOR

Be mentored and mentor others. Your team will meet weekly with a mentor to set goals and celebrate victories. They will work closely with you, providing resources and inspiration. Most importantly, your mentor will help ensure you are on track to meet your goals.

FEAST

We'll hold two weekly meals and invite guest experts in to talk about their favorite subjects. Talk SASS over stew and privacy over pancakes. Each Friday we'll rehash the week over a third meal, allowing you to customize the upcoming schedule based on what your team needs.

BUILD

Start by working on small projects to serve needs in the community. Take on increasingly challenging work with the help of experienced collaborators. Over the year, build a portfolio of your work and start to contribute to open-source projects. Serious hours are set aside to work on projects.

WORKSHOP

Tinker and learn new skills. Expert guests will join us multiple times per week to help you dive into new languages and overcome tricky learning challenges. Workshops won't just be on code. We will learn about desk-building, logic, poetry, and occupational health as well.

DISCUSS

Exchange ideas and start to ask hard questions about your work. Read important texts on the past, present and future of the internet, on hacker culture, and autodidacticism. You'll be expected to write every week and discuss critical questions concerning the future of the open web.



RESOURCES & COMMUNITY, NOT LECTURES & STUDENTS

The schedule for the year is, to some degree, up to you and what you want to build. We'll provide the best community and resources we can, but we've realized that if we plan your year for you, you won't be working towards what's next. As Calvin says to Miss Wormwood, "You can present the material, but you can't make me care." It's kind of like getting a bulletin in a church service or reading the translation at the Opera. You become a passive audience member with your attention on the content, not your experience. The success of your year requires serious engagement. We want you to help you start your life's work now. Of course, there is something valuable about being able to answer the question "What should I learn next?" We won't give you an entire schedule for the year, but we'll give you a Fieldbook on day one. Instead of setting your year in stone, this resource will give you some good ideas on first projects, what to learn next, some texts to read, and highlight some core principles. Think of it as a really awesome "Hacker Scout" handbook : ) Not only will the Fieldbook be packed full of great resources, it will provide a basic structure for the year's learning; it will help you set goals for your work and visualize your progress. Sorry, we're not going to give you any badges for your sash, but we do hope the Fieldbook finds a permanent spot in your knapsack and gives you an inspiring picture of what's possible with code. Below is a brief outline of the fieldbook: a three-part guide for starting and an overview of its resources. It's a brief picture of everything you could learn throughout the year. The Fieldbook is, in no small part, inspired by the Girl and Boy Scouts, ESR's "How to Become a Hacker" and why's "(poignant) guide to ruby".

THE FIELDBOOK

I. STARTING

Learn what things are and how they work: computer, os, program, server, client, language, internet, url. Build a personal site from scratch and setup your workspace: get git and software, build a desk, etc. Challenge yourself to find the best help and to fall in love with code. Read hacker FAQs.

II. WHAT'S NEXT

Learn to use Terminal, publish your personal blog using Git, dig into HTML/CSS, and one dynamic, object-oriented language. Start building a web platform using what you've learned. Challenge yourself to use design methods in your work and study UX. Start re-writing parts of this fieldbook.

III. KEEP GOING

Learn C and get to know UNIX. Work with another object-oriented language, multiple web frameworks, and JS/JQuery. Help the community build an open-source project by becoming a consistent contributor. Start your own project with a team. Challenge yourself to write weekly and fall in love with a problem you want to solve.


IV. PRACTICE

A guide of the best resources for learning the technical skills you need.

Git, Terminal, Systems/Servers, C, Ruby, Python, web frameworks, JS/JQuery, CSS, SASS/LESS, HTML, Databases, APIs, Testing, etc.


V. PROJECTS

A guide full of little projects to learn on and ideas for what to build next.

"Hello World", yourself.com, "Terminal for a Week", Open-Source projects to start on, Arduino, Raspberry Pi, build a web server, etc.


VI. WONDER

A guide and compendium of texts that inform the way we work and why.

Logic, design theory, poetry, mathematics, history of computation, history of the web, hacker culture, open source theory, privacy, security, economics, culture, comic books, games, education, etc.

GOALS

1. Dev.PROJECTS

You will start the year working in our Dev.Clinic on pro-bono client projects and move quickly to building your own ideas. You'll form teams and work with experienced mentors to build web apps. Become a serious contributing member of an open-source project.

2. PORTFOLIO

By the end of the year, we want you to be certified by the community you serve. Your client work, portfolio of projects, github and stack overflow profiles, and writing collection will be a record of your work. Your portfolio will also reflect some of the personal goals you've set for your year.

3. FULLER-STACK

A year isn't long enough to master anything, but we want to introduce you to the full stack and then go further by including: Theory and Humanities, Graphic Design, GIS/Mapping, and Human-Centered Design.

4. DON'T STOP

Once the year is over, keep going. While we won't give you the same dedicated attention, you're still part of the community. Stay with us as a mentor. Come to meals and workshops. Keep coding with your team and start a company (maybe an open company!).

5. THEORY

Study Hacker Culture, Open Source Theory and Economics, Writing, issues of Labor and Equity in technology, Poetry, Logic, Euclidean Geometry, and Applied Philosophy. After a year, you should have built up a serious collection of your work.

6. FOCAL EXPERIENCES

These are the events in the year that will challenge you as a person and help you become a better leader, entrepreneur, and learner. Practice improvisation, wilderness survival, desk-building, cooking, and artistic inquiry.
